# Independent Review — EN Version

**Reviewed:** 2026-04-15
**Reviewer lens:** British defence-community native speaker

---

## Overall assessment

The English text is solidly readable and clearly above the quality threshold for international publication. The argumentative structure (seven acts, escalating from "what is this?" to "what follows?") survives the language well, and the Churchill section lands with appropriate irony. The two main weaknesses are (1) a handful of slightly stiff or over-literal constructions that signal a non-native hand, and (2) one terminological inconsistency around "overseas missions" vs. the established NATO/UK term "out-of-area operations." Nothing is a blocker, but a handful of fixes — especially in Act 2 and Act 6 — would make the piece indistinguishable from native output.

---

## High-priority findings (must fix before publication)

**H1 — Line 267 (Act 1, closing paragraph)**
> "The report is submitted by the Bundestag in plenary context."

"In plenary context" is not a natural English phrase; it is a calque of German *im Plenum* or *im parlamentarischen Kontext*. In English the standard phrasing is "tabled in a plenary sitting" or simply "presented to the full Bundestag." The sentence should also clarify who *submits* vs. who *receives* it — the Commissioner submits it *to* the Bundestag; the Bundestag does not submit it.

**Suggested fix:**
> "The report is presented to the Bundestag in plenary session. The Parliamentary Commissioner is elected by Parliament and maintains an independent staff."

---

**H2 — Line 284 (Act 2)**
> "In 1957 the Bundestag passed the Act on the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Armed Forces."

Fine in isolation, but immediately followed by:
> "The legal basis: Article 45b of the Basic Law, inserted in 1956."

The two-sentence sequence creates a logical paradox for an informed English reader: the enabling statute was passed in 1957, but its constitutional basis was "inserted" in 1956. The word "inserted" is an unusual choice here — constitutions are *amended* to add articles. Standard English would say "added by amendment in 1956" or "incorporated into the Basic Law in 1956."

**Suggested fix:**
> "The legal basis is Article 45b of the Basic Law, added by constitutional amendment in 1956 — one year before the enabling statute."

---

**H3 — Line 286 (Act 2)**
> "In 2025 those were 2,819 personal submissions, which generated 4,254 formal proceedings covering a total of 6,960 individual matters."

"Personal submissions" is used throughout the piece for *Eingaben*, but in the hero figure (line 189) the label is "Personal submissions 2025." For an English-speaking defence reader, "personal submissions" sounds odd — one might assume it means submissions *about* personnel rather than submissions *by* soldiers as individuals. The German legal term is closer to "petitions" or "individual complaints." The official Bundestag English translation of the report uses "petitions." Using "petitions" (or "individual petitions") throughout would be both more accurate and more natural.

**Suggested fix:** Replace all instances of "personal submissions" with "individual petitions" (or simply "petitions"). Keep the hero label consistent: "Individual petitions 2025."

---

**H4 — Line 352, Act 4**
> "France, the Netherlands, Sweden, Finland, Norway, and Denmark have parliamentary defence committees that produce thematic reports."

Norway appears in this list, but Norway is also correctly identified (line 694, chart data) as having a "select_committee" model — a meaningfully different category from the generic "parliamentary commission" of the other countries in the list. Grouping Norway with France, Netherlands, etc. in the prose creates an inconsistency with what the chart shows. Norway's committee (Utenriks- og forsvarskomiteen) is a combined Foreign Affairs and Defence committee, not a pure defence committee. The prose should either note Norway's different model or re-examine the grouping.

**Suggested fix:**
> "France, the Netherlands, Sweden, Finland, and Denmark have parliamentary defence committees that produce thematic reports. Norway's combined Foreign Affairs and Defence Committee operates similarly."

## Medium-priority findings (should fix)

**M1 — Line 204 (Act 1)**
> "On 3 March 2026, Henning Otte submitted the 67th Annual Report of the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Armed Forces. Bundestag printed paper 21/4200."

"Bundestag printed paper" is a literal translation of *Bundestag-Drucksache*. The official English term used by the Bundestag itself is "Bundestag printed paper" — this is technically correct but looks odd standalone in the middle of a sentence. Consider either hyphenating it as a compound ("Bundestag printed paper 21/4200") — which is what you have — or rendering it more naturally: "Reference: BT-Drs. 21/4200." Many English readers in the parliamentary/defence world would recognise the "BT-Drs." abbreviation from EU context, and a parenthetical might clarify.

**Suggested fix:**
> "On 3 March 2026, Henning Otte submitted the 67th Annual Report of the Parliamentary Commissioner for the Armed Forces (Bundestag printed paper 21/4200). 96 pages."

Minor, but the fragment "96 pages." standing alone is stylistically punchy and deliberate — it works; keep it.

---

**M2 — Line 229 (KPI tile, Act 1)**
> "in 9 mandated overseas missions"

In NATO and UK MoD usage, "overseas missions" is correct for *Auslandseinsätze*, but the standard term in UK parliamentary and defence writing is "overseas operations" or "out-of-area operations." "Missions" is more of a US/NATO usage. Since the chart (line 651) uses "overseas missions" and the Act 4 text uses "overseas missions" consistently throughout, this is internally consistent — but consider whether "overseas operations" would better suit the British/NATO audience. If you change it, change all instances together.

---

**M3 — Line 304 (Act 2)**
> "This is not a scandal. This is not a war. This is precisely the kind of everyday problem for which — without the Parliamentary Commissioner — there would be no channel: no line manager, no procurement office, no form."

"No line manager" is slightly off: a line manager *exists* for every soldier; the point is that the line manager is part of the chain of command from which the soldier cannot seek redress. The intended meaning is "no route outside the chain of command." Consider: "no channel outside the chain of command: not a line manager, not a procurement office, not a form."

**Suggested fix:**
> "This is precisely the kind of everyday problem for which — without the Parliamentary Commissioner — there would be no independent channel: no route past the line manager, no procurement office to petition, no form to fill in."

---

**M4 — Line 348 (Act 4)**
> "Polly Miller-Perkins took office on 30 March 2026."

This is presented as a fact without any hedging. At time of writing (April 2026), verify that this name and date are correct — the Act passed in March 2025 but the appointment process was separate. If accurate, no change needed. If not yet confirmed, add "reportedly" or "as announced by Parliament." (This is flagged as a terminological risk, not a content fact-check per se — if the name is misspelled or the date is wrong, it undermines credibility with the British defence audience, who will know this institution.)

---

**M5 — Line 379 (Act 5)**
> "Germany rose from 1.24% (2015) to 2.18% of GDP (2024) — the NATO target was exceeded for the first time in 2024."

In standard English prose, the NATO target is written as "the NATO 2% target" or "NATO's 2% of GDP target" — not just "the NATO target" without specifying which target. For readers unfamiliar with the context, "the NATO target" could mean anything. Since the piece is aimed at a defence-aware audience, this is acceptable but reads slightly loose.

**Suggested fix:**
> "— exceeding NATO's 2% of GDP target for the first time in 2024."

---

**M6 — Line 390 (Act 5)**
> "2016 marked the low point at 176,000."

Technically "low point" is correct, but in military contexts "nadir" or "historic low" reads more naturally, particularly in a British defence context. Minor stylistic preference.

---

**M7 — Line 406 (Act 6)**
> "A model without public stocktaking works — it simply demands a different currency: democratic legitimacy."

"Demands a different currency: democratic legitimacy" — this metaphor is slightly tangled. A "currency" is something you spend or use; saying democratic legitimacy is the "currency" demanded implies you spend democratic legitimacy to maintain the model, which is not the intended meaning. The intended meaning is: the model *costs* democratic legitimacy, or *comes at the price of* democratic legitimacy.

**Suggested fix:**
> "A model without public stocktaking works — it simply comes at a different price: democratic legitimacy."

---

**M8 — Line 408 (Act 6)**
> "When the individual soldier has no longer any complaints channel that can bypass the chain of command."

The word order "has no longer any" is a germanism (*hat keinen ... mehr*). Natural English is "no longer has any" or "no longer has a."

**Suggested fix:**
> "When the individual soldier no longer has any complaints channel that bypasses the chain of command."

## Low-priority / nitpicks

**L1 — Line 193 (hero figure)**

The "a" before "report" is weak — it implies there might be multiple reports. Since this is clearly about *the* report established in Act 1, write: "Every single figure appears in the report."

**L2 — Line 225 (KPI tile)**
> "€62.3bn ... (+10)"

The delta "(+10)" is missing the currency symbol and unit. It should read "(+€10bn)" or "(+10bn)" for consistency with the other deltas. As rendered, a reader momentarily reads it as "+10 percentage points" or "+10 units" before context clarifies.

**L3 — Line 267 (Act 1)**
> "The institution has seen 14 office-holders in 67 years"

Count the names in the chart data: von Grolman, Heye, Hoogen, Schultz, Berkhan, Weiskirch, Biehle, Marienfeld-Czesla, Penner, Robbe, Königshaus, Bartels, Högl, Otte = 14. Correct. No change needed, but flag for fact-check if the data ever changes.

---

**L4 — Line 282 (Act 2)**
> "Wolf Graf von Baudissin developed the concept of *Innere Führung* (leadership development and civic education)"

The standard English rendition of *Innere Führung* in UK/NATO contexts is "leadership development and civic education" (used by the Bundeswehr itself), or more often left in German as *Innere Führung* with a brief gloss. The piece's approach is fine. The word "civic" could alternatively be "civil" — "civic education" implies citizenship education in schools; "leadership development and civic responsibility" might be marginally clearer. Minor.

---

**L5 — Line 335 (Act 4 section title)**
> "Barely anyone. But the UK is copying it right now."

"Copying" has a slightly pejorative connotation in British English (implies slavish imitation, possibly even plagiarism). For a section describing a legitimate policy transfer that the UK itself describes as learning from the German model, consider: "adapting it" or "following suit." The German-defence-community audience may read "copying" as diminishing the UK's deliberate policy decision.

**Suggested fix:**
> "Barely anyone. But the UK is adopting it right now."

---

**L6 — Line 400 (Act 6, section label)**
> "The transparency trap sounds like a problem. But it has another side:"

"Transparency trap" is used here as if it has been established earlier in the text — but the piece never explicitly names or introduces a "transparency trap." The reader encounters the term for the first time in Act 6's lead paragraph. Either introduce the concept earlier ("what critics call a transparency trap") or rephrase here.

**Suggested fix:**
> "Critics frame this openness as a transparency trap — a risk that public disclosure of shortcomings emboldens adversaries. But it has another side:"

---

**L7 — Chart labels (Chart 6, Personnel, line 845)**
> `label:{formatter:'1990 · Unification'}`

In British/NATO English, the event in 1990 is "German reunification" or "German unification" — just "Unification" without qualifier is slightly ambiguous at chart-label size. Given the space constraints of a rotated chart label, "Reunification" (one word, 13 chars) fits and is unambiguous.

**Suggested fix:** Change `'1990 · Unification'` to `'1990 · Reunification'`.

## Notable strengths

- **The Churchill passage (lines 321–327)** is the piece's rhetorical centrepiece and it lands cleanly in English. The contrast between oratory and data is understated in exactly the right way; the follow-up paragraph ("What Churchill did not say: how many Spitfires were still flying…") is crisp and effective. No changes needed.

- **Act 2 structure** — the explanation of *why* the institution exists (Reichswehr/Wehrmacht history → Innere Führung → constitutional grounding → Section 7 protection from retaliation) is logically tight and does not patronise the informed reader. The pivot from "public transparency" to "protection for individual people in uniform" (line 286) is the piece's best argumentative move.

- **Number formatting** is entirely consistent throughout: comma thousand separators, period decimals, British spellings (organisation, visualised). This is the most common failure point in translated data stories and this piece handles it cleanly.

- **The section title "Not naivety. History."** (Act 2, line 275) is excellent. It captures the full argumentative arc of the section in four words. Do not touch it.

- **Act 6 (transparency → pressure → budget → reform loop)** is well-reasoned and avoids the typical editorial trap of either naively celebrating transparency or catastrophising about adversary exploitation. The Polish counter-example is deployed deftly.

- **Institutional terminology** (Parliamentary Commissioner for the Armed Forces, GAO, Armed Forces Commissioner Act, NATO Eastern Flank) is used correctly and consistently throughout. The piece will read as credible to a specialist audience.
